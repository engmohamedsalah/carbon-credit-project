Carbon Credit Verification Glossary

This glossary explains key terms used throughout the Carbon Credit Verification SaaS application and documentation. Terms are organized alphabetically for easy reference.

Why This Glossary Matters

Carbon credit verification involves many specialized terms from different fields like forestry, remote sensing, climate science, and blockchain technology. This glossary helps you understand these terms without needing to be an expert in all these areas.

If you encounter an unfamiliar term while using the application or reading the documentation, check here first!

A

Additionality

Simple explanation: The requirement that carbon credits only come from projects that wouldn't have happened without carbon credit funding.

Technical definition: A key criterion for carbon credit projects requiring that greenhouse gas emission reductions or removals would not have occurred in the absence of the carbon credit market incentive.

API (Application Programming Interface)

Simple explanation: A way for different software programs to talk to each other and share information.

Technical definition: A set of rules and protocols that allows one software application to interact with another, enabling data exchange and functionality sharing between systems.

B

Baseline

Simple explanation: The starting point against which changes are measured, like taking a "before" picture.

Technical definition: The reference scenario that represents the greenhouse gas emissions or removals that would have occurred in the absence of the carbon project.

Blockchain

Simple explanation: A digital record-keeping system where information is stored across many computers, making it very difficult to change or fake.

Technical definition: A distributed, immutable ledger technology that records transactions across multiple computers in a way that ensures data cannot be altered retroactively without altering all subsequent blocks.

Biomass

Simple explanation: The total amount of living material in an area, like trees, plants, and other vegetation.

Technical definition: The total mass of living organic matter in a specific area, often used to estimate carbon storage in forests and other ecosystems.

C

Carbon Credit

Simple explanation: A permit that represents one ton of carbon dioxide that has been removed from the atmosphere or prevented from being released.

Technical definition: A tradable certificate or permit representing the right to emit one metric ton of carbon dioxide or equivalent greenhouse gas.

Carbon Sequestration

Simple explanation: The process of capturing and storing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, like how trees absorb CO2 as they grow.

Technical definition: The process of capturing and storing atmospheric carbon dioxide through biological, chemical, or physical processes to mitigate climate change.

Carbon Sink

Simple explanation: A natural environment that absorbs more carbon than it releases, like a growing forest.

Technical definition: A reservoir that accumulates and stores carbon-containing compounds for an indefinite period, reducing the concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere.

D

Double Counting

Simple explanation: When the same carbon credit is counted more than once, like two people claiming credit for the same good deed.

Technical definition: A situation where a single greenhouse gas emission reduction or removal is counted more than once towards achieving climate mitigation targets or goals.

E

Explainable AI (XAI)

Simple explanation: Artificial intelligence systems that can explain their decisions in ways humans can understand.

Technical definition: A set of processes and methods that allows human users to comprehend and trust the results and output created by machine learning algorithms.

F

Forest Cover

Simple explanation: The amount of land covered by forests in a specific area.

Technical definition: The percentage or area of land covered by tree canopy as observed from above, typically measured using remote sensing techniques.

G

GeoJSON

Simple explanation: A format for storing geographic information that computers can easily understand.

Technical definition: An open standard format designed for representing simple geographical features, along with their non-spatial attributes, based on JavaScript Object Notation.

H

Human-in-the-Loop

Simple explanation: A system where AI makes initial decisions, but humans review and can override them when needed.

Technical definition: A model that requires human interaction to validate machine learning outputs, combining automated processing with human judgment to ensure accuracy and reliability.

I

Indigenous Data Sovereignty

Simple explanation: The right of indigenous peoples to control data about their lands, communities, and cultural knowledge.

Technical definition: The right of indigenous peoples to govern the collection, ownership, and application of data about their communities, peoples, lands, and resources.

M

Machine Learning (ML)

Simple explanation: Computer systems that learn from examples rather than being explicitly programmed.

Technical definition: A subset of artificial intelligence that enables systems to learn and improve from experience without being explicitly programmed, using algorithms to identify patterns in data.

MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, Verification)

Simple explanation: The three-step process of tracking carbon projects: measuring what's happening, documenting it, and confirming it's accurate.

Technical definition: A framework used to measure greenhouse gas emissions, compile this information in reports, and subject these reports to review or audit to ensure accuracy and reliability.

N

NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index)

Simple explanation: A measurement that shows how green and healthy plants are in satellite images.

Technical definition: A numerical indicator that uses the visible and near-infrared bands of the electromagnetic spectrum to analyze remote sensing measurements and assess whether the target being observed contains live green vegetation.

P

Permanence

Simple explanation: The requirement that carbon stored by a project stays stored for a long time, not just temporarily.

Technical definition: A criterion for carbon offset projects requiring that the greenhouse gas reductions or removals are maintained over time and not reversed.

Polygon (Blockchain)

Simple explanation: An energy-efficient blockchain system that works with Ethereum but uses less energy and has lower fees.

Technical definition: A Layer 2 scaling solution for Ethereum that provides faster transactions and lower fees while maintaining compatibility with Ethereum's ecosystem.

R

Remote Sensing

Simple explanation: Collecting information about the Earth from a distance, usually using satellites or aircraft.

Technical definition: The science of obtaining information about objects or areas from a distance, typically from aircraft or satellites, by detecting and measuring radiation of different wavelengths reflected or emitted from objects or surface areas.

S

Sentinel-2

Simple explanation: A pair of European satellites that take detailed pictures of Earth's surface every few days.

Technical definition: A European Space Agency Earth observation mission that provides multispectral imagery with 13 spectral bands at 10-60m resolution and a revisit time of 5 days with two satellites.

Smart Contract

Simple explanation: A digital agreement that automatically executes when certain conditions are met, without needing a middleman.

Technical definition: Self-executing contracts with the terms directly written into code, automatically enforcing agreements when predetermined conditions are met on a blockchain.

V

Verification

Simple explanation: The process of checking that carbon reductions or removals are real, accurate, and meet all the required standards.

Technical definition: The independent assessment of reported greenhouse gas emission reductions or removals to determine if they are accurate, complete, consistent, transparent, and without material misstatements.

How to Use This Glossary

Throughout the application and documentation, you'll see terms from this glossary highlighted. Hover over these terms to see a quick definition, or click to jump to the full explanation in this glossary.

If you encounter terms that aren't in this glossary but should be, please let us know so we can continue to improve our documentation.
